Welcome to the Anglosphere, Kate. Congratulations on learning English to the level you have. I can tell you're good because first, I can understand you, and second, the only errors I can identify are very hard to explain.

I'm a language-geek, so I'll try anyway.

"keep up with" (Definition 1: "move at the same rate as something" or metaphorically "do with the same skill as something") (Definition 2: "keep current knowledge of something" "pay attention to" "follow")

"With" must be omitted if there is no direct object:
ex: Don't worry. I can keep up.

"With" must be included if there is a direct object.
ex: He talks too fast. I can't keep up with him.

Finally, if "with" is separated, the "keep up" cannot have a direct object.

ex: I can't keep up in conversations with a native speaker.
("keep up" form.)

ex: I can't keep up with a native speaker in conversations.

If you like, I can try to explain the difference between "a conversation," "the conversation," "conversation," and so on, but probably the best person to help would be another Russian who has learned English.

Best of luck.

I'm glad you understood it. When I start talking about something that interests me, like computers, math, or language, I sometimes lose people in the jargon. Oops.

Anyway, I'd like to respond to the content of your post now.

Probably the best sources of practice are books, movies, TV shows, and so on. It is more important to listen and read than it is to speak or write. Don't take my word for it, though. Check out

http://antimoon.com

by Tomasz Szynalski and Michal Wojcik. They aren't lying when they say they're good. Tomasz's pronunciation is perfect. He sounds like he grew up in the US. Michal has a noticeable accent, but it sounds good and doesn't distract from what he's saying. They both write at a near-native level.

The only thing I really disagree with is their recommendation of SuperMemo. I've used both SuperMemo and Anki, and I have to recommend Anki. It uses the same review scheduling method as SuperMemo, but has much better support for non-latin letters and is easer to use and is free.

http://ichi2.net/anki/index.html

If you have a reasonably fast Internet connection, you can use Livestation to watch TV on your computer. There are *lots* of English-language channels, especially British and American. There are even some Japanese channels, too.

http://livestation.com

For books, I mostly order from

http://amazon.com

(or visit one of their affiliated stores). http://amazon.co.uk is the British version of their site, if you prefer British English. Many older books (before 1920 or so) can often be downloaded for free from

http://www.gutenberg.org/ .

Another thing Antimoon mentions is switching to an English-only dictionary if you haven't already. They recommend some paper dictionaries, but you might want to try

http://learnersdictionary.com and http://dictionary.com

since they're convenient and free.

Finally, I want to mention Khatzumoto's All Japanese All the Time. It's a more fun, more "how-to" version of Antimoon with a particular emphasis on learning Japanese.

http://www.alljapaneseallthetime.com/blog/about

Hope you have fun.
